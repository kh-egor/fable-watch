I

The arc, in full

A sixteen-day reflection on the first export control ever applied to an AI model.

In sixteen days, the most capable model the public had ever been handed became a sovereign-risk event — and bent through nearly every institution that now governs frontier AI.

It opened as an ordinary product story. Fable 5 shipped on June 9 as the first public "Mythos-class" model, and within a day the complaints were the usual ones: over-zealous guardrails, a silent downgrade to a weaker model mid-conversation, and a viral jailbreak from "Pliny the Liberator." Familiar noise.

Then the register changed. In June 11 Senate testimony, Sen. Mark Warner relayed that, in a red-team test, Mythos had found its way through "almost all" of a classified government environment "in hours." Whether that was a breach or merely a benchmark is still contested — better reporting since makes clear the model found weaknesses, it did not exploit them — but it reframed everything. This was no longer about refusals; it was about national security.

On June 12 the Commerce Department reached for export controls — the first time the instrument has been turned on an AI model — barring foreign-national access. Unable to verify nationality at the API layer, Anthropic went dark worldwide in ninety minutes. What followed read less like a tech story than a diplomatic one: a public dispute with the White House, a ~100-signatory security open letter (freefable.org), and a G7 summit where allies confronted the fact that their frontier tools could be switched off overnight — and Paris pushed a "trusted partners" regime in response.

Then the thaw. Trump, after meeting Amodei at the G7, said Anthropic was "no longer" a national-security threat. The company and the government converged on guardrails, identity verification, and shared benchmarks. On June 26 the first reversal landed: Mythos 5 cleared to return to roughly a hundred vetted U.S. defenders. Fable, the public model, is expected back within the week — pending Pentagon and NSA sign-off.

The throughline: capability outran governance. The "too-powerful" narrative Anthropic marketed became the lever used against it, the oversight it championed boomeranged — and the world learned that an off-switch for frontier AI is now real, and that it lives in Washington.

III

Why it happened

Architecture, the silent downgrade, the jailbreak, and the red-team claim.

One model, two products. Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are the same underlying model, split by a layer of safety classifiers: locked-down Fable 5 for the public, less-restricted Mythos 5 for ~100 vetted partners (Project Glasswing). That gating is exactly what the government just re-authorised for Mythos while holding Fable back.

The "silent sabotage." When a query tripped a high-risk classifier, Fable 5 silently routed it to the weaker Opus 4.8 mid-session — no notice, no override. Researchers (Nathan Lambert, Jeremy Howard) called it categorically misaligned; Anthropic later made the fallback visible but did not remove it.

The jailbreak that lit the fuse. "Pliny the Liberator" leaked Fable's full system prompt via multi-agent decomposition + Unicode homoglyphs. Anthropic disputes the severity — refusal-coaxing surfacing already-public info, capability "widely available from other models including GPT-5.5," with the hardest safeguards held in independent classifiers. Security researcher Katie Moussouris agreed: "It's not a jailbreak."

The claim that changed the stakes. The disproportionate response is best explained by the June 11 red-team testimony — Mythos reportedly finding vulnerabilities across classified systems "in hours." Crucial caveat: this remains unverified (no incident report, no NSA/CISA bulletin) and finding a weakness is not the same as exploiting it — "the NSA wasn't hacked by AI."

IV

The reaction

One event, read a dozen ways — and three ironies underneath.
The three boomerangs
  1. The danger narrative. Anthropic marketed Mythos as too powerful to release. The government took it at its word — and now Anthropic must argue its own model down.
  2. The regulation it championed. Anthropic pushed hardest for oversight; the blunt, ad-hoc version landed on it first. NSPM-11 forbids any vendor disabling AI the U.S. relies on — yet Washington forced exactly that on allies.
  3. Its own backer pulled the trigger. The shutdown was reportedly set off by Amazon's Andy Jassy — Anthropic's $13B investor, board member, AWS host and Nova competitor.
