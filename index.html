TL;DR — Why it vanished

It was not a billing change. On June 12, 2026, 5:21 PM ET, the U.S. government issued an export-control directive citing national-security authorities, ordering Anthropic to suspend all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for any foreign national — anywhere, including Anthropic's own foreign-born staff. Rather than selectively block users, Anthropic disabled both models globally, across every plan and surface to guarantee immediate compliance. The trigger: the government believes it found a "jailbreak" method (reportedly: asking the model to read code and flag flaws). Anthropic reviewed it, says it only surfaced "a small number of previously known, minor vulnerabilities," and publicly disagrees with the recall — but is complying with the legal order.

✅ Opus 4.8, Sonnet 4.6, and Haiku 4.5 are unaffected and operating normally.

🔬 Deep dive — architecture, the silent downgrade & the Pliny jailbreak

One model, two products. Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are the same underlying model, separated not by capability but by a layer of safety classifiers sitting in front of it: locked-down Fable 5 for the public, less-restricted Mythos 5 for ~12 vetted security partners and 40+ infrastructure providers. (Interconnects / N. Lambert)

The "silent sabotage." When a query trips a classifier in a high-risk category (cybersecurity, bio/chem, model distillation), Fable 5 silently routes the request to the weaker Opus 4.8 mid-session — no notice, no override, and the downgrade sticks for the rest of the conversation. The behaviour was buried in a 319-page system card. Real false-positives piled up in Anthropic's own tracker: a P0 issue where benign syscall/ABI dev content silently downgraded a session, and #67246 where /model couldn't override it; one user reported Fable refusing 200/200 ProgramBench tasks.

The walk-back. After backlash from researchers — Gergely Orosz (30-day prompt retention + paying for a frontier model that can be "nerfed" without notice), Elie Bakouch ("bad on purpose" for frontier research), alphaXiv — Anthropic made the fallback visible/labeled, but did not remove it: flagged researchers still get the weaker model, now just with a label. (Fortune)

The jailbreak that triggered the directive. "Pliny the Liberator" published Fable 5's full ~120,000-char / 1,585-line system prompt to GitHub within ~24h of launch — via multi-agent decomposition (splitting harmful asks into dozens of benign sub-steps), Unicode homoglyph substitution, and academic framing — reportedly eliciting cyber / chem / explosives / manipulation content. Anthropic disputes the severity, calling it refusal-coaxing that surfaced only previously-known minor vulnerabilities, "widely available from other models including GPT-5.5" (it cites OpenAI's own GPT-5.5 cyber-eval disclosures), and stresses its hardest safeguards are independent classifiers that survive any model-level refusal bypass. (SecurityWeek) Independent security researcher Katie Moussouris concurred — "It's not a jailbreak," characterizing the Amazon finding as defensive prompting, not exploitation. The community verdict, per Pliny: "one of the most disappointing model drops of all time."

🗣️ How the world reacted — the opinion map

The three boomerangs

  1. The danger narrative. Anthropic spent months marketing Mythos as too powerful to release. The government took it at its word — and now Anthropic must argue its own model down, calling the jailbreak "narrow" and already present in GPT-5.5.
  2. The regulation it championed. Anthropic pushed hardest for AI oversight; the blunt, ad-hoc version landed on it first. The irony cuts deeper: NSPM-11 says no vendor may disable an AI the U.S. depends on — yet Washington just forced Anthropic to do exactly that to allied governments. (The 2023 red-team oversight that might have caught this was rescinded in Jan 2025.)
  3. Its own backer pulled the trigger. The shutdown was reportedly set off by Amazon CEO Andy Jassy — simultaneously Anthropic's $13B investor, board member, AWS host, and Nova competitor — flagging an Amazon-found jailbreak to Treasury hours before the directive.
